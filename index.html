1. Vector Analysis

Vector Fundamentals & Products

A scalar is a physical quantity with magnitude only (mass, time). A vector possesses both magnitude and direction (force, velocity). Vectors are represented in 3D Cartesian coordinates using unit vectors iˆ, jˆ, and kˆ.

General Vector: A = Axiˆ + Ayjˆ + Azkˆ
Magnitude: |A| = √(Ax² + Ay² + Az²)
Unit Vector (Direction): Aˆ = A / |A|

Dot Product (Scalar Product)

The result of a dot product is a scalar number. It tells us how much of one vector goes in the direction of another.

A · B = |A||B| cos(θ)

A · B = AxBx + AyBy + AzBz

Orthogonality Principle: If two vectors are perpendicular to each other (θ = 90°), their dot product is strictly zero: A · B = 0.

Cross Product (Vector Product)

The result of a cross product is a new vector that is perfectly perpendicular to the plane formed by the original two vectors.

A × B = |A||B| sin(θ) nˆ

Parallel Principle: If two vectors are parallel or anti-parallel (θ = 0° or 180°), their cross product is zero: A × B = 0.

📝 Numerical: Orthogonal Vectors
Problem: Given vectors A = 2iˆ - 3jˆ + 7kˆ and B = 5iˆ + 1jˆ + Xkˆ. Find X if vectors A and B are orthogonal.
Solution:
1. Condition for orthogonality: A · B = 0
2. Apply dot product: (2)(5) + (-3)(1) + (7)(X) = 0
3. Simplify: 10 - 3 + 7X = 0
4. Solve for X: 7 + 7X = 0 ⇒ 7X = -7 ⇒ X = -1
📝 Numerical: Finding Components
Problem: A plane is 100 km from the airport in a direction 30° South of East. If +x is East and +y is North, find its position vector.
Solution:
Magnitude r = 100 km. East is +x, South is -y.
x = r cos(30°) = 100 × √3 / 2 = 86.6 km
y = -r sin(30°) = -100 × 0.5 = -50 km
Position vector r = 86.6iˆ - 50jˆ km
2. Forces & Mechanics

Newton's Laws & Friction Dynamics

Newton's First Law: A body remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by a net external force.
Newton's Second Law: The rate of change of linear momentum is directly proportional to the applied force: ΣF = ma.
Newton's Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

Friction

Friction is a non-conservative contact force that always opposes relative motion between two surfaces.

  • Static Friction (fs): Adjusts itself to prevent motion. It has a maximum limit called limiting friction: fs ≤ μsN
  • Kinetic Friction (fk): Constant force acting when the object is actively sliding: fk = μkN (Note: μk < μs)
📝 Numerical: Block Pulled at an Angle
Problem: A 10 kg block is pulled by a 20 N force at an angle of 30° to the horizontal. Find the Normal force (N) given g = 10 m/s².
Solution:
Gravity force downward: Mg = 10 × 10 = 100 N.
Vertical component of applied force: F sin(30°) = 20 × 0.5 = 10 N (upwards).
Applying Newton's 2nd Law in Y-direction (ΣFy = 0):
N + 10 - 100 = 0 ⇒ N = 90 N
📝 Numerical: Pulley System
Problem: Find the acceleration of an ideal pulley system with masses M and m (where M > m).
Solution:
For mass M (moving down): Mg - T = Ma
For mass m (moving up): T - mg = ma
Adding equations: Mg - mg = Ma + ma ⇒ a = g(M - m) / (M + m)
⚙️ Derivation: Acceleration on a Rough Inclined Plane

Consider a block of mass 'm' sliding down a rough inclined plane making an angle θ with the horizontal. Coefficient of kinetic friction is μk.

Step 1: Resolve gravitational force (mg) Downward along the incline: Fparallel = mg sin(θ)
Perpendicular into the incline: Fperpendicular = mg cos(θ)
Step 2: Determine Normal Force (N) Since there is no vertical bouncing off the plane, N balances the perpendicular component:
N = mg cos(θ)
Step 3: Define Kinetic Friction (fk) Friction acts up the incline to oppose the downward sliding motion:
fk = μkN = μk(mg cosθ)
Step 4: Apply Newton's Second Law along the plane ΣF = ma
(Force pulling down) - (Friction pulling up) = ma
mg sin(θ) - μkmg cos(θ) = ma
Final Result: Cancel 'm' from all terms:
a = g [ sin(θ) - μkcos(θ) ]
3. Momentum, Work & Energy

Conservation Laws and Theorems

Linear Momentum & Impulse

Linear momentum (P) is defined as P = mv. If the net external force is zero, momentum is conserved (Pinitial = Pfinal).

Impulse (I): The total effect of a large force acting over a short time (like a bat hitting a ball). It is the area under the Force-Time curve.

I = ∫ F dt = Favg × Δt
Impulse-Momentum Theorem: I = ΔP = Pfinal - Pinitial
📝 Numerical: Impulse on a Football Player
Problem: A 70 kg player running at 36 km/hr (10 m/s) is hit and bounces back at 18 km/hr (5 m/s). Contact time is 0.05s. Find Impulse and Average Force.
Solution:
Velocities: vi = -10 m/s (initial direction), vf = 5 m/s (bounce back direction).
Impulse = m(vf - vi) = 70 × [5 - (-10)] = 70 × 15 = 1050 N·s
Average Force = I / Δt = 1050 / 0.05 = 21,000 N
📝 Numerical: 1D Collision & Recoil
Problem 1: Car A (1000 kg, 5 m/s) rear-ends Car B (2000 kg, 2 m/s). After collision, Car A moves at 3 m/s. Find Car B's final speed.
Problem 2: A 15-g (0.015 kg) bullet is fired from a 5 kg rifle at a muzzle velocity of 600 m/s. Find the recoil velocity of the rifle.
Solution 1:
m1v1i + m2v2i = m1v1f + m2v2f
(1000)(5) + (2000)(2) = (1000)(3) + 2000(v2f)
5000 + 4000 = 3000 + 2000v2f ⇒ 6000 = 2000v2f ⇒ v2f = 3 m/s

Solution 2:
Initial momentum = 0. Therefore: 0 = Mvrifle + mvbullet
0 = 5(vrifle) + 0.015(600) ⇒ 5(vrifle) = -9 ⇒ vrifle = -1.8 m/s

Work & The Work-Energy Theorem

Work is defined as the dot product of Force and Displacement: W = ∫ F · ds.

⚙️ Derivation: The Work-Energy Theorem

Statement: The net work done by all forces on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy.

Step 1: Start with the definition of Work in 1D W = ∫ F dx
Step 2: Apply Newton's Second Law F = ma = m(dv/dt)
Step 3: Chain Rule for Acceleration a = (dv/dx) × (dx/dt) = v(dv/dx)
Therefore, F = m [ v(dv/dx) ]
Step 4: Substitute into the Work integral W = ∫ [m v (dv/dx)] dx = ∫ m v dv
Step 5: Integrate from initial velocity (u) to final velocity (v) W = m [ v² / 2 ] evaluated from u to v
W = ½mv² - ½mu²
Final Result: Wnet = KEfinal - KEinitial = ΔKE
📝 Numerical: Work-Energy on a Rough Surface
Problem: A 1 kg block at rest is pulled by a 34 N horizontal force over 1 m. The friction coefficient μk is 0.2. Find its final speed (g=10).
Solution:
Friction force fk = μkmg = 0.2 × 1 × 10 = 2 N.
Net force = 34 N - 2 N = 32 N.
Work done by net force W = 32 N × 1 m = 32 J.
Work-Energy theorem: W = ΔKE ⇒ 32 = ½(1)v² ⇒ v² = 64 ⇒ v = 8 m/s
📝 Numerical: Energy Conservation on Incline
Problem: A 5 kg block (at rest) is released from a height of 20 m on a frictionless incline. Calculate the height (x) at which the kinetic energy is 3 times the potential energy.
Solution:
Total initial mechanical energy at top = mg(20).
At height x: PE = mgx and KE = 3(mgx).
Total energy at x = mgx + 3mgx = 4mgx.
Conservation of energy: 4mgx = mg(20) ⇒ 4x = 20 ⇒ x = 5 m
📝 Numerical / Derivation: The Ballistic Pendulum
Problem: A bullet of mass m1 and velocity vA is fired into a large wooden block of mass m2 suspended by strings. The bullet embeds in the block, and the entire system swings up to a maximum height h. Derive the formula to find the initial speed of the bullet vA.
Methodology (Two-Part Physics Problem):
Part 1: The Collision (Conservation of Momentum)
Right before collision, only the bullet moves. Right after, they move together with velocity vB.
m1vA + m2(0) = (m1 + m2)vB
Equation 1: vB = (m1vA) / (m1 + m2)

Part 2: The Swing (Conservation of Mechanical Energy)
After the collision, the block+bullet system swings upward. Kinetic energy at the bottom converts entirely to Potential energy at height h.
KEbottom = PEtop
½(m1 + m2)vB² = (m1 + m2)gh
Cancel masses: ½vB² = gh ⇒ vB = √(2gh)

Part 3: Substitute to find vA
Substitute vB back into Equation 1:
√(2gh) = (m1vA) / (m1 + m2)
Final Formula: vA = [ (m1 + m2) / m1 ] × √(2gh)
1. Wave Fundamentals

Traveling Waves & Equations

A traveling wave is a disturbance that carries energy from one place to another without transferring matter. Mathematically, a 1D transverse harmonic wave traveling in the +x direction is described by:

Wave Equation: y(x,t) = ym sin(kx - ωt + φ)
Where:
• ym = Amplitude (maximum displacement)
• k = Angular wave number = 2π / λ
• ω = Angular frequency = 2πf = 2π / T
• φ = Initial phase angle
Wave Speed (v): v = λf = ω / k
Note: If the wave travels in the -x direction, the equation uses a plus sign: y(x,t) = ym sin(kx + ωt + φ).
📝 Numerical: Traveling Wave Equation
Problem: A transverse wave on a string is given by y(x,t) = 0.05 sin(40πx - 200πt). Where x and y are in meters and t in seconds. Find the amplitude, wavelength, frequency, and wave speed.
Solution:
Compare with standard form: y(x,t) = ym sin(kx - ωt)
1. Amplitude (ym) = 0.05 m
2. k = 40π ⇒ 2π/λ = 40π ⇒ λ = 2/40 = 0.05 m
3. ω = 200π ⇒ 2πf = 200π ⇒ f = 100 Hz
4. Speed (v) = ω / k = 200π / 40π = 5 m/s
2. Superposition of Waves

The Principle of Superposition

When two or more waves travel through the same medium simultaneously, the net displacement of the medium at any point is the algebraic sum of the individual displacements. ynet = y1 + y2 + ... + yn

Interference

Interference is a direct result of superposition. When two waves of the same frequency and constant phase difference overlap:

  • Constructive Interference: Waves arrive in-phase (crests align with crests). Resultant amplitude is maximized.
    Path Difference: Δx = nλ (where n = 0, 1, 2...)
  • Destructive Interference: Waves arrive completely out-of-phase (crests align with troughs). Resultant amplitude is minimized.
    Path Difference: Δx = (n + ½)λ
Phase Difference (Δφ) vs Path Difference (Δx):
Δφ = ( 2π / λ ) × Δx

4. Wave Optics

Young's Double Slit Experiment (YDSE)

Interference is the phenomenon where two waves superimpose to form a resultant wave of greater, lower, or the same amplitude. Strict Conditions: The two sources must be coherent (maintaining a constant phase difference) and monochromatic (single wavelength).

⚙️ Derivation: Path Difference in YDSE

Consider two slits S1 and S2 separated by distance 'd'. A screen is placed at distance 'L' (where L >> d). A point P on the screen is at distance 'y' from the central axis.

Step 1: Geometric Path Difference (δ) The wave from S2 travels further than S1 to reach P. If we drop a perpendicular from S1 to S2P, the extra distance is δ = S2P - S1P.
Step 2: Small Angle Approximation Because L is very large, the rays are nearly parallel. The angle θ made by the point P from the center of the slits gives:
δ ≈ d sin(θ)
Step 3: Relate to Screen Position (y) For small angles, sin(θ) ≈ tan(θ) = y / L.
Therefore, Path Difference δ = (y · d) / L

Conditions for Fringes

YDSE (Interference) Bright Fringes

Constructive Condition: δ = mλ (where m = 0, ±1, ±2...)

Position: ybright = (mλL) / d

YDSE (Interference) Dark Fringes

Destructive Condition: δ = (m + ½)λ

Position: ydark = (m + ½)λL / d

Fringe Width (β): The distance between any two consecutive bright or dark fringes.
β = λL / d
📝 Numerical: YDSE Calculation
Problem: In a YDSE, the screen is placed 1.5m away. The slits are 0.05 mm apart. If the third bright fringe is 4.5 cm from the center, calculate the wavelength of light.
Solution:
y = (mλL) / d
Convert units: y = 0.045 m, L = 1.5 m, d = 5 × 10&supmin;&sup5; m, m = 3.
λ = (y · d) / (m · L)
λ = (0.045 × 5 × 10&supmin;&sup5;) / (3 × 1.5)
λ = 2.25 × 10&supmin;&sup6; / 4.5 = 5 × 10&supmin;&sup7; m = 500 nm
5. Diffraction

Fraunhofer Single Slit Diffraction

Diffraction is the bending of light around the corners of an obstacle. Unlike interference (two distinct sources), diffraction in a single slit occurs due to the interference of secondary wavelets originating from different parts of the same wavefront.

Fresnel Diffraction

Source and screen are at finite distances from the obstacle. Wavefronts are spherical or cylindrical. Math is complex.

Fraunhofer Diffraction (Exam Focus)

Source and screen are effectively at infinity (achieved using convex lenses). Wavefronts are planar. Math is simpler.

The Single Slit Minima (Dark Fringes)

In a single slit of width 'a', the condition for destructive interference is surprisingly similar to the constructive condition for YDSE. Do not confuse them!

Dark Fringe Condition: a sin(θ) = mλ   (where m = ±1, ±2, ±3... but never 0)
Width of Central Maximum: W = 2λL / a

Note: The central fringe (m=0) is the brightest and widest. Its width is exactly double the width of all the secondary maxima.

FeatureInterference (YDSE)Diffraction (Single Slit)
Fringe WidthAll fringes have exactly equal width.Central maximum is double the width of secondary fringes.
IntensityAll bright fringes are equally intense.Intensity falls rapidly from the center outwards.
📝 Numerical: Single Slit Central Maximum
Problem: Light of wavelength 500 nm falls on a single slit of width 0.1 mm. The screen is 2m away. What is the width of the central maximum?
Solution:
Formula: Width = 2λL / a
Given: λ = 500 × 10&supmin;&sup9; m, L = 2m, a = 1 × 10&supmin;&sup4; m.
Width = (2 × 500×10&supmin;&sup9; × 2) / (1×10&supmin;&sup4;)
Width = (2000×10&supmin;&sup9;) / (1×10&supmin;&sup4;) = 2000×10&supmin;&sup5; = 0.02 m = 2 cm

2. The Photoelectric Effect

Detailed Theory & Graphical Analysis

The photoelectric effect is the emission of electrons from a metal surface when illuminated by light of a sufficiently high frequency. Classical wave theory failed to explain this because:

  1. Independence of Intensity: Classical theory predicted higher intensity light would increase electron Kinetic Energy. Experiment showed KE is independent of intensity.
  2. Threshold Frequency: Classical theory predicted any frequency would work eventually. Experiment showed a strict cutoff frequency (ν0) below which NO emission occurs.
  3. Instantaneous Emission: Classical theory predicted a time delay for energy buildup at low intensities. Experiment showed emission is instantaneous (< 10&supmin;&sup9; s).
Einstein's Photoelectric Equation:
Ephoton = Φ + KEmax
hν = hν0 + KEmax

Ultimate Shortcut Formula:
E(eV) = 1240 / λ(nm)

Key Graphs in the Photoelectric Effect

1. Current vs Intensity

If frequency is constant, the photoelectric current is directly proportional to the intensity of light. More intensity = more photons = more ejected electrons.

2. Current vs Voltage

At a specific frequency and intensity, as retarding voltage increases, current drops to zero at the Stopping Potential (V0). Different intensities yield the same V0.

3. V0 vs Frequency (ν)

A straight line plot.
Equation: V0 = (h/e)ν - (Φ/e).
Slope: h/e (constant for all metals).
X-Intercept: Threshold frequency ν0.

📝 Numerical: Photoelectric Emission
Problem: Light of wavelength 200 nm falls on an aluminum surface (work function Φ = 4.2 eV). Calculate the kinetic energy of the fastest emitted electrons and the stopping potential.
Solution:
1. Calculate Photon Energy (E) using the shortcut:
E = 1240 / λ(nm) = 1240 / 200 = 6.2 eV

2. Calculate Maximum Kinetic Energy:
KEmax = E - Φ = 6.2 eV - 4.2 eV = 2.0 eV

3. Calculate Stopping Potential (V0):
Since KEmax = eV0, the stopping potential is simply the KE value in Volts.
V0 = 2.0 Volts
📝 Numerical: Stopping Potential Shift
Problem: The stopping potential of a surface is 3V at 500nm. Find the stopping potential V2 at 600nm.
Solution:
V = 1240/λ - Φ/e
V1 - V2 = 1240 × (1/500 - 1/600) = 1240 × (0.002 - 0.00166) = 1240 × 0.000333
V1 - V2 = 0.41 V
V2 = 3 - 0.41 = 2.59 V
1. Fundamentals

Electric Charge & Coulomb's Law

Charge is quantized: Q = ±ne (where n is an integer, and e = 1.602 × 10&supmin;¹&sup9; C). Like charges repel; opposite charges attract.

Coulomb's Law (Force between point charges):
F = [ 1 / (4πε0) ] · [ |q1q2| / r² ] rˆ
Where ε0 (permittivity of free space) = 8.854 × 10&supmin;¹² C²/(N·m²).
The constant k = 1 / (4πε0) ≈ 9 × 10&sup9; N·m²/C².

Electric Field (E)

The force experienced by a unit positive test charge placed in the field: E = F / q0. For a point charge, E = k|q|/r².

2. Gauss's Law

Electric Flux and Theorem

Electric Flux (ΦE) measures the number of electric field lines passing through a given surface area. Mathematically, it is the dot product of the Electric Field vector and the Area vector: ΦE = ∫ E · dA.

Gauss's Law Statement:
The net electric flux through any closed surface (Gaussian surface) is equal to the net charge enclosed divided by the permittivity of free space.
∮ E · dA = Qenclosed / ε0
⚙️ Derivation 1: Electric Field of an Infinite Line of Charge

Consider an infinitely long straight wire with a uniform linear charge density λ (Charge per unit length).

Step 1: Choose Gaussian Surface By symmetry, the electric field points radially outward. We choose a cylinder of radius 'r' and length 'l' coaxial with the wire.
Step 2: Calculate Flux (∮ E · dA) The cylinder has 3 parts: Top flat cap, bottom flat cap, and curved surface.
- Top & Bottom caps: The Area vector points vertically, while E points radially outward. Angle is 90°. Since cos(90°)=0, Flux = 0.
- Curved surface: E is parallel to dA (Angle = 0°, cos(0)=1). Area of curved surface is 2πrl.
Total Flux ΦE = E(2πrl).
Step 3: Calculate Enclosed Charge The length of wire enclosed by our cylinder is 'l'.
Qencl = λ · l
Step 4: Apply Gauss's Law E(2πrl) = (λl) / ε0
Final Result: Cancel 'l' to get: E = λ / (2πε0r)
⚙️ Derivation 2: Electric Field of an Infinite Plane Sheet

Consider an infinite, thin, non-conducting sheet with uniform surface charge density σ (Charge per unit area).

Step 1: Choose Gaussian Surface We choose a cylindrical "pillbox" piercing through the sheet. Let the cross-sectional area of the flat caps be 'A'.
Step 2: Calculate Flux - Curved surface: E field is perpendicular to the area vector. Flux = 0.
- Two flat caps: E field points outward, parallel to dA. Flux through one cap = EA. Since there are two caps (front and back), Total Flux ΦE = 2EA.
Step 3: Calculate Enclosed Charge The pillbox cuts a circle of area A out of the sheet.
Qencl = σ · A
Step 4: Apply Gauss's Law 2EA = (σA) / ε0
Final Result: Cancel 'A' to get: E = σ / (2ε0)
Note: The electric field is uniform and does not depend on distance 'r'!
3. Capacitors

Potential & Parallel Plate Capacitance

A capacitor stores electrical potential energy. Capacitance (C) measures how much charge it can store per unit of voltage: C = Q / V. Measured in Farads (F).

⚙️ Derivation: Capacitance of a Parallel Plate Capacitor

Consider two parallel conducting plates of Area A, separated by distance d. One plate has charge +Q, the other -Q. Surface charge density σ = Q/A.

Step 1: Electric Field between plates Between two oppositely charged plates, the fields add up: E = σ/(2ε0) + σ/(2ε0) = σ / ε0.
Substituting σ = Q/A gives: E = Q / (ε0A).
Step 2: Potential Difference (V) Since the field is uniform, Voltage is Field times distance: V = E · d.
Substituting E: V = (Q · d) / (ε0A).
Step 3: Calculate Capacitance C = Q / V
C = Q / [ (Q · d) / (ε0A) ]
Final Result: Cancel Q to get: C = ε0A / d

Energy Stored: U = ½CV² = Q² / (2C)

📝 Numerical: Parallel-Plate Capacitor Calculation
Problem: The plates of a parallel-plate capacitor in a vacuum are 5.00 mm apart and have an area of 2.00 m². A 10.0-kV potential difference is applied across the capacitor. Compute (a) the capacitance and (b) the charge on each plate.
Given: d = 5 × 10&supmin;³ m, A = 2.00 m², V = 10,000 V.

(a) Capacitance:
C = ε0A / d
C = (8.85 × 10&supmin;¹² × 2) / (5 × 10&supmin;³)
C = 17.7 × 10&supmin;¹² / 0.005 = 3.54 × 10&supmin;&sup9; F = 3.54 nF

(b) Charge:
Q = C × V
Q = (3.54 × 10&supmin;&sup9;) × (10 × 10³)
Q = 35.4 × 10&supmin;&sup6; C = 35.4 μC
1. Magnetic Fundamentals

Magnetic Fields & Forces

Unlike electric charges, magnetic monopoles do not exist. If you break a bar magnet in half, you get two smaller dipoles, each with a North and South pole. Magnetic field lines always form closed continuous loops, pointing from North to South externally, and South to North internally.

Lorentz Force on a Moving Charge

A stationary charge experiences no magnetic force. A charge 'q' moving with velocity 'v' in a magnetic field 'B' experiences a force given by the cross product:

FB = q (v × B)
Magnitude: FB = qvB sin(θ)
Total Lorentz Force (Electric + Magnetic): F = q(E + v × B)
⚙️ Derivation: Radius of a Charged Particle in a Magnetic Field

If a charged particle enters a uniform magnetic field perpendicular to its velocity (θ = 90°), the magnetic force is perpendicular to the velocity, acting as a centripetal force causing uniform circular motion.

Step 1: Equate Forces Magnetic Force = Centripetal Force
qvB sin(90°) = m(v² / r)
Step 2: Solve for Radius (r) qvB = mv² / r
Cancel one 'v': qB = mv / r
Final Result: r = mv / (qB)

Observation: Faster or heavier particles have larger radii. Stronger fields or higher charges cause tighter (smaller) radii.

2. Sources of Magnetic Field

Biot-Savart & Ampere's Law

The Biot-Savart Law gives the infinitesimal magnetic field dB produced by a small current element Idl. It is the magnetic equivalent of Coulomb's Law.
dB = (μ0 I / 4π) · (dl × rˆ) / r²

Ampere's Law Statement:
The line integral of the magnetic field around any closed loop is equal to μ0 times the net steady current enclosed by the loop. It is the magnetic equivalent of Gauss's Law, highly useful for symmetric current distributions.
∮ B · dl = μ0 Ienclosed
Where μ0 (permeability of free space) = 4π × 10&supmin;&sup7; T·m/A
⚙️ Derivation 1: Magnetic Field of an Infinite Straight Wire

Consider an infinitely long straight wire carrying a steady current I. We want to find the magnetic field B at a radial distance 'r'.

Step 1: Choose Amperian Loop By symmetry, the magnetic field lines form concentric circles around the wire (Right-Hand Rule). We choose a circular loop of radius 'r' centered on the wire.
Step 2: Evaluate the Line Integral At every point on the circular loop, B is tangential, and the path element dl is also tangential. Therefore, angle θ = 0°, cos(0) = 1.
∮ B · dl = ∮ B dl = B ∮ dl
The integral of dl around a circle is its circumference (2πr).
∮ B · dl = B(2πr)
Step 3: Apply Ampere's Law B(2πr) = μ0 Ienclosed
Since the loop encloses the entire wire, Iencl = I.
Final Result: B = μ0I / (2πr)
⚙️ Derivation 2: Magnetic Field Inside an Ideal Solenoid

A solenoid is a long coil of wire. For an "ideal" solenoid (infinitely long, tightly packed), the magnetic field is uniform and parallel to the axis inside, and practically zero outside.

Let the solenoid have 'N' total turns over a length 'L'. Let 'n' be the number of turns per unit length (n = N/L). The current is 'I'.

Step 1: Choose Amperian Loop We choose a rectangular loop (1-2-3-4) of length 'l' and width 'w'. Side 1 is perfectly parallel to the axis inside the solenoid. Side 3 is outside the solenoid. Sides 2 and 4 cut across the coils perpendicular to the field.
Step 2: Evaluate the Line Integral in four parts ∮ B · dl = ∫1 B·dl + ∫2 B·dl + ∫3 B·dl + ∫4 B·dl

- Side 1 (Inside): B and dl are parallel. Integral = B · l
- Sides 2 & 4 (Crossing): B is perpendicular to dl (dot product = 0). Integral = 0
- Side 3 (Outside): B ≈ 0 outside an ideal solenoid. Integral = 0

Summing them up: ∮ B · dl = Bl + 0 + 0 + 0 = Bl
Step 3: Calculate Enclosed Current The rectangular loop encloses a certain number of wire turns. Since there are 'n' turns per unit length, a length 'l' encloses (n × l) turns. Each turn carries current I.
Iencl = (n × l) × I
Step 4: Apply Ampere's Law Bl = μ0 (n · l · I)
Final Result: Cancel the length 'l' on both sides to get:
B = μ0 n I
(Or B = μ0(N/L)I ). Notice the field is independent of the solenoid's radius!
