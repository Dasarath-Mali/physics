Unit I: Classical Mechanics

1. Vector Analysis & Operations

A scalar is a physical quantity with magnitude only (mass, time, temperature). A vector possesses both magnitude and direction (force, velocity, acceleration). Vectors are represented in 3D Cartesian coordinates using unit vectors iˆ, jˆ, and kˆ.

General Vector Formulation: A = Axiˆ + Ayjˆ + Azkˆ
Vector Magnitude: |A| = √(Ax² + Ay² + Az²)
Unit Vector (Direction only): Aˆ = A / |A|

Dot Product (Scalar Product)

The dot product yields a scalar quantity. It measures how much of one vector goes in the direction of another (parallel projection).

A · B = |A||B| cos(θ)

A · B = AxBx + AyBy + AzBz

Orthogonality Principle: If two vectors are strictly perpendicular to each other (θ = 90°), their dot product is exactly zero: A · B = 0.

Cross Product (Vector Product)

The cross product yields a new vector that is perfectly perpendicular to the plane formed by the original two vectors, determined by the Right-Hand Rule.

A × B = |A||B| sin(θ) nˆ

Parallel Principle: If vectors are parallel or anti-parallel, their cross product is zero: A × B = 0.

📝 Numerical: Orthogonal Vectors
Problem: Given two vectors A = 2iˆ - 3jˆ + 7kˆ and B = 5iˆ + 1jˆ + Xkˆ. Find the value of X if vectors A and B are orthogonal to each other.
Solution:
1. Condition for orthogonality: A · B = 0
2. Apply dot product formula: (2)(5) + (-3)(1) + (7)(X) = 0
3. Simplify the equation: 10 - 3 + 7X = 0
4. Solve for X: 7 + 7X = 0 ⇒ 7X = -7 ⇒ X = -1
📝 Numerical: Finding Vector Components
Problem: An airplane is located 100 km from the airport in a direction 30° South of East. If +x represents East and +y represents North, find the airplane's position vector.
Solution:
Magnitude r = 100 km. East is positive x, South is negative y.
x-component = r cos(30°) = 100 × (√3 / 2) = 100 × 0.866 = 86.6 km
y-component = -r sin(30°) = -100 × 0.5 = -50.0 km
Position vector r = 86.6iˆ - 50.0jˆ km

2. Linear & Rotational Dynamics

Newton's Laws & Friction

Newton's First Law (Inertia): A body remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by a net external force.
Newton's Second Law: The rate of change of linear momentum is proportional to the applied force. ΣF = ma = dp/dt.
Newton's Third Law: Every action has an equal and opposite reaction.

  • Static Friction (fs): A variable force that adjusts to prevent relative motion. Maximum limit: fs ≤ μsN
  • Kinetic Friction (fk): A constant opposing force during active sliding: fk = μkN (Always μk < μs)
⚙️ Derivation: Acceleration on a Rough Inclined Plane

Consider a block of mass 'm' sliding down a rough inclined plane making an angle θ with the horizontal. Coefficient of kinetic friction is μk.

Step 1: Resolve gravitational force (mg) Downward along the incline: F|| = mg sin(θ)
Perpendicular into the incline: F⊥ = mg cos(θ)
Step 2: Determine Normal Force (N) & Friction Since the block doesn't bounce off the plane, forces perpendicular to the plane must balance: N = mg cos(θ)
Kinetic friction acts UP the plane to oppose downward motion: fk = μkN = μkmg cos(θ)
Step 3: Apply Newton's Second Law along the plane ΣF = ma
(Force pulling down) - (Friction pulling up) = ma
mg sin(θ) - μkmg cos(θ) = ma
Final Result: Cancel 'm' from all terms to yield the acceleration:
a = g [ sin(θ) - μkcos(θ) ]
📝 Numerical: Block Pulled at an Angle
Problem: A 10 kg block is pulled across a floor by a 20 N force acting at an angle of 30° above the horizontal. Find the Normal force (N) acting on the block. Assume g = 10 m/s².
Solution:
1. Calculate downward gravity force: W = mg = 10 kg × 10 m/s² = 100 N.
2. Calculate vertical component of the applied force (lifting the block slightly): Fy = F sin(30°) = 20 × 0.5 = 10 N (upwards).
3. Apply equilibrium in the Y-direction (ΣFy = 0):
Normal Force (Up) + Applied Vertical Force (Up) - Gravity (Down) = 0
N + 10 N - 100 N = 0 ⇒ N - 90 N = 0 ⇒ N = 90 N
📝 Numerical: The Atwood Machine (Pulley System)
Problem: Find the general expression for the acceleration of an ideal, frictionless pulley system with two hanging masses M and m (where M > m).
Solution:
Since M is heavier, it will accelerate downwards at 'a', and m will accelerate upwards at 'a'. Both share the same string tension 'T'.
Equation for heavy mass M (moving down): Mg - T = Ma
Equation for light mass m (moving up): T - mg = ma
Add the two equations together to eliminate T:
(Mg - T) + (T - mg) = Ma + ma
Mg - mg = a(M + m)
a = g(M - m) / (M + m)

Rotational Dynamics

Rotational motion perfectly mirrors linear motion. Mass (inertia) becomes Moment of Inertia (I), Force becomes Torque (τ), and Linear Momentum becomes Angular Momentum (L).

Torque (τ): τ = r × F. Also, Στ = Iα (Rotational Newton's 2nd Law).
Angular Momentum (L): L = r × p. Also, L = Iω.
Conservation of Angular Momentum: If the net external torque is zero (Στext = 0), then the total angular momentum of the system is conserved: Linitial = Lfinal (or I1ω1 = I2ω2).

3. Momentum, Collisions & Center of Mass

Center of Mass (COM)

The unique point where the entire mass of a system can be assumed to be concentrated for the purpose of analyzing translational motion.

For Discrete Particles: Rcm = (m1r1 + m2r2 + ... ) / (m1 + m2 + ...)
For a Continuous Body: Rcm = (1/M) ∫ r dm

Impulse & 1D Collisions

Impulse (I): The total change in momentum resulting from a force acting over a period of time. Visually, it is the area under a Force vs. Time graph.

I = ∫ F dt = Favg × Δt

Impulse-Momentum Theorem: I = ΔP = Pfinal - Pinitial

2D Oblique Collisions

When objects collide at an angle (not head-on), the law of conservation of momentum applies independently to the X and Y axes.

X-axis Conservation: m1u1x + m2u2x = m1v1x + m2v2x
Y-axis Conservation: m1u1y + m2u2y = m1v1y + m2v2y
📝 Numerical: Impulse on a Football Player
Problem: A 70 kg football player running at 36 km/hr (10 m/s) is hit by a defender and bounces back at 18 km/hr (5 m/s). The contact time of the hit is 0.05s. Find the total Impulse and the Average Force exerted on the player.
Solution:
1. Establish sign convention: Let the initial running direction be negative, and the bounce-back direction be positive.
Initial velocity (vi) = -10 m/s.
Final velocity (vf) = +5 m/s.

2. Calculate Impulse (Change in Momentum):
Impulse = m(vf - vi) = 70 kg × [ 5 m/s - (-10 m/s) ] = 70 × 15 = 1050 N·s

3. Calculate Average Force:
Favg = Impulse / Δt = 1050 / 0.05 s = 21,000 N
📝 Numerical: 1D Collisions & Rifle Recoil
Problem A: Car A (mass 1000 kg, velocity 5 m/s) rear-ends Car B (mass 2000 kg, velocity 2 m/s) moving in the same direction. After the collision, Car A slows to 3 m/s. Find Car B's final speed.

Problem B: A 15-g (0.015 kg) bullet is fired from a 5 kg rifle at a muzzle velocity of 600 m/s. Find the recoil velocity of the rifle.
Solution A (Car Collision):
m1v1i + m2v2i = m1v1f + m2v2f
(1000)(5) + (2000)(2) = (1000)(3) + 2000(v2f)
5000 + 4000 = 3000 + 2000v2f
9000 = 3000 + 2000v2f ⇒ 6000 = 2000v2f ⇒ v2f = 3 m/s

Solution B (Rifle Recoil):
Before firing, the system is at rest. Initial momentum = 0.
0 = Mvrifle + mvbullet
0 = 5(vrifle) + (0.015)(600)
0 = 5(vrifle) + 9 ⇒ 5(vrifle) = -9 ⇒ vrifle = -1.8 m/s (The negative sign indicates recoil opposite to the bullet).

4. Work, Energy & Variable Mass

Work is defined as the dot product of Force and Displacement: W = ∫ F · ds. If the force is constant, W = F d cos(θ).

⚙️ Derivation: The Work-Energy Theorem

Statement: The net work done by all forces on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy.

Step 1: Start with the 1D Work Integral W = ∫ F dx
Step 2: Apply Newton's 2nd Law Replace F with mass × acceleration.
F = ma = m(dv/dt)
Step 3: Chain Rule for Acceleration We can rewrite acceleration with respect to position 'x' instead of time 't'.
a = (dv/dx) × (dx/dt). Since dx/dt is velocity (v), we get a = v(dv/dx).
Therefore, F = m [ v(dv/dx) ]
Step 4: Substitute into the Work integral W = ∫ [m v (dv/dx)] dx. The 'dx' cancels out.
W = ∫ m v dv
Step 5: Integrate from initial velocity (u) to final velocity (v) W = m [ v² / 2 ] evaluated from u to v
W = ½mv² - ½mu²
Final Result: Wnet = KEfinal - KEinitial = ΔKE
⚙️ Derivation: Variable Mass System (Rocket Propulsion)

A rocket accelerates forward in deep space by burning fuel and ejecting mass backward at a relative exhaust velocity vrel.

Step 1: Setup Conservation of Momentum Since no external forces act in deep space, dp/dt = 0. Momentum is constant.
Momentum at time t: P(t) = mv
Momentum at time t+dt: The rocket's mass decreases to (m - dm) and velocity increases to (v + dv). The ejected exhaust gas has mass 'dm' and velocity (v - vrel).
P(t+dt) = (m - dm)(v + dv) + dm(v - vrel)
Step 2: Expand and equate P(t) = P(t+dt) mv = [ mv + mdv - vdm - dmdv ] + [ vdm - vreldm ]
Ignore the tiny 2nd order differential term (dmdv ≈ 0).
mv = mv + mdv - vdm + vdm - vreldm
Cancel 'mv' and 'vdm' terms on both sides:
0 = mdv - vreldm
Step 3: Rearrange and Integrate mdv = vreldm ⇒ dv = vrel (dm / m)
Integrate from initial velocity v0 to final velocity v, and initial mass m0 to final mass m.
∫ dv = vrel ∫ (1/m) dm
Final Result (Tsiolkovsky Rocket Equation): v - v0 = vrel ln(m0 / m)

Thrust Force: By rearranging mdv = vreldm and dividing by dt, we find the thrust force propelling the rocket: Fthrust = vrel (dm/dt)
📝 Numerical: Work-Energy on a Rough Surface
Problem: A 1 kg block initially at rest is pulled by a 34 N horizontal force over a distance of 1 m. The coefficient of kinetic friction μk is 0.2. Find the final speed of the block (Assume g=10 m/s²).
Solution:
1. Calculate friction force: fk = μkmg = 0.2 × 1 kg × 10 m/s² = 2 N.
2. Calculate net pulling force: Fnet = Applied Force - Friction = 34 N - 2 N = 32 N.
3. Calculate net work done: Wnet = Fnet × displacement = 32 N × 1 m = 32 J.
4. Apply Work-Energy theorem: Wnet = ΔKE
32 J = ½mv² - 0
32 = ½(1)v² ⇒ v² = 64 ⇒ v = 8 m/s
📝 Numerical: Energy Conservation on a Frictionless Incline
Problem: A 5 kg block is released from rest from a height of 20 m on a frictionless inclined surface. Calculate the specific height (x) from the ground at which the block's kinetic energy is exactly 3 times its potential energy.
Solution:
1. Total initial mechanical energy at the top (only potential): Einitial = mgh = mg(20).
2. At the unknown height x, the energy is split between PE and KE.
PE = mgx.
We are given that KE = 3 × PE = 3mgx.
Therefore, total energy at height x: Efinal = PE + KE = mgx + 3mgx = 4mgx.
3. Apply Conservation of Energy (Einitial = Efinal):
mg(20) = 4mgx
Cancel 'mg': 20 = 4x ⇒ x = 5 m
📝 Numerical / Derivation: The Ballistic Pendulum
Problem: A bullet of mass m1 and unknown initial velocity vA is fired into a large wooden block of mass m2 suspended by strings. The bullet embeds completely in the block, and the entire system swings upward to a maximum height h. Derive the formula to determine the initial speed of the bullet vA based on height h.
Methodology (A Classic Two-Part Physics Problem):

Part 1: The Inelastic Collision (Conservation of Momentum)
Right before the collision, only the bullet is moving. Right after, the bullet and block move together as one mass with a new velocity vB. Kinetic energy is lost here as heat/deformation, so we can ONLY use momentum.
m1vA + m2(0) = (m1 + m2)vB
Equation 1: vB = (m1vA) / (m1 + m2)

Part 2: The Upward Swing (Conservation of Mechanical Energy)
After the collision is over, the block+bullet system swings upward. Now, friction/deformation is over, so mechanical energy is conserved. The kinetic energy at the bottom converts entirely to potential energy at the peak height h.
KEbottom = PEtop
½(m1 + m2)vB² = (m1 + m2)gh
Cancel the combined mass from both sides: ½vB² = gh ⇒ vB = √(2gh)

Part 3: Substitute to solve for vA
Substitute the result for vB back into Equation 1:
√(2gh) = (m1vA) / (m1 + m2)
Rearrange to isolate vA:
Final Formula: vA = [ (m1 + m2) / m1 ] × √(2gh)

Unit II: Optics & Waves

1. Traveling Waves & Superposition

A traveling wave is a disturbance that carries energy and momentum from one place to another without permanently transferring the underlying matter. Mathematically, a 1D transverse harmonic wave traveling in the positive x-direction is described by the sine function:

Standard Traveling Wave Equation: y(x,t) = ym sin(kx - ωt + φ)

Where the variables represent:
• ym = Amplitude (maximum vertical displacement from equilibrium)
• k = Angular wave number = 2π / λ (Measured in rad/m)
• ω = Angular frequency = 2πf = 2π / T (Measured in rad/s)
• φ = Initial phase angle (determines starting position at t=0)

Wave Speed (v): v = λf = ω / k
Direction Rule: If the wave equation has a minus sign (kx - ωt), it travels to the RIGHT (+x direction). If it has a plus sign (kx + ωt), it travels to the LEFT (-x direction).
📝 Numerical: Extracting Data from a Wave Equation
Problem: A transverse wave on a string is described by the equation y(x,t) = 0.05 sin(40πx - 200πt). Where x and y are measured in meters and t is in seconds. Find the amplitude, wavelength, frequency, and wave propagation speed.
Solution:
Compare the given equation with the standard form: y(x,t) = ym sin(kx - ωt)
1. Amplitude (ym) = 0.05 m
2. Wavelength (λ): We see k = 40π. Since k = 2π/λ, we get 2π/λ = 40π ⇒ λ = 2/40 = 0.05 m
3. Frequency (f): We see ω = 200π. Since ω = 2πf, we get 2πf = 200π ⇒ f = 100 Hz
4. Wave Speed (v): v = λf = (0.05 m) × (100 Hz) = 5 m/s (Alternatively, v = ω/k = 200π / 40π = 5 m/s).

The Principle of Superposition

When two or more waves travel through the same medium simultaneously, they do not bounce off each other. Instead, they pass through one another. The net displacement of the medium at any given point is the algebraic sum of the individual wave displacements: ynet = y1 + y2 + ... + yn.

Interference

Interference is the physical consequence of superposition when two waves of the same frequency and constant phase difference overlap.

  • Constructive Interference: Waves arrive completely in-phase (crests align with crests, troughs with troughs). The resultant amplitude is the sum of the individual amplitudes, creating a maximum.
    Condition: Path Difference Δx = nλ (where n = 0, 1, 2, 3...)
  • Destructive Interference: Waves arrive completely out-of-phase (crests align with troughs). They cancel each other out, creating a minimum or zero amplitude.
    Condition: Path Difference Δx = (n + ½)λ
Relationship between Phase Difference (Δφ) and Path Difference (Δx):
Δφ = ( 2π / λ ) × Δx

2. The Electromagnetic Spectrum

Electromagnetic (EM) waves are transverse waves consisting of oscillating electric and magnetic fields that are perpendicular to each other and to the direction of travel. Unlike mechanical waves, EM waves require no medium and travel through a vacuum at the absolute speed limit of the universe: c = 3 × 10&sup8; m/s.

The spectrum below is ordered by Increasing Wavelength (which means Decreasing Frequency and Decreasing Energy per photon).

Region Wavelength Range Key Characteristics & Applications
Gamma Rays (γ) < 0.01 nm Highest energy, highest frequency. Emitted by radioactive nuclear decay. Used in targeted cancer radiation treatment.
X-Rays 0.01 nm - 10 nm High energy, highly penetrating. Used for medical bone imaging, airport security, and crystallography.
Ultraviolet (UV) 10 nm - 400 nm Can cause sunburn and cellular DNA damage. Used for water sterilization, counterfeit detection, and fluorescent lighting.
Visible Light 400 nm - 700 nm The narrow band the human eye can perceive. Ranges from Violet (~400nm) to Red (~700nm).
Infrared (IR) 700 nm - 1 mm Felt as heat radiation. Used in night vision goggles, thermal imaging cameras, and TV remote controls.
Microwaves 1 mm - 1 m Used in radar systems, satellite communications, Wi-Fi, and microwave ovens (which resonate water molecules).
Radio Waves > 1 m Lowest energy, longest wavelength. Easily diffracts around buildings. Used for TV and AM/FM radio broadcasting.

3. Wave Optics: Interference & Diffraction

Young's Double Slit Experiment (YDSE)

Thomas Young's famous 1801 experiment definitively proved the wave nature of light. For the interference pattern to be stable and visible, the two light sources must be:

  1. Coherent: They must maintain a constant phase difference over time. You cannot use two separate lightbulbs because they emit light randomly. You must use a single laser split into two slits.
  2. Monochromatic: They must consist of a single wavelength (one specific color). If white light is used, the fringes overlap and create a smeared rainbow pattern.
⚙️ Derivation: Path Difference in YDSE

Consider two slits S1 and S2 separated by a very small distance 'd'. A viewing screen is placed at a much larger distance 'L' (L >> d). A point P on the screen is located at a vertical distance 'y' from the central central axis.

Step 1: Geometric Path Difference (δ) The light wave traveling from the lower slit S2 has to travel further than the wave from S1 to reach point P. If we drop a perpendicular line from S1 down to the path S2P, the extra trailing distance is the path difference: δ = S2P - S1P.
Step 2: Small Angle Approximation Because the screen distance L is massive compared to the slit gap d, the two light rays are practically parallel. A geometric analysis shows that the angle θ made by point P from the center point between the slits forms a right triangle at the slits. Thus:
Path difference δ ≈ d sin(θ)
Step 3: Relate to Screen Position (y) For small angles (which is true for fringes near the center), sin(θ) is roughly equal to tan(θ). Looking at the large triangle formed by the screen, tan(θ) = opposite / adjacent = y / L.
Substituting this back: δ = d(y / L)
Therefore, Path Difference δ = (y · d) / L

Fringe Conditions in YDSE

YDSE Bright Fringes (Maxima)

Occur when the path difference causes constructive interference.

Condition: δ = mλ (where m = 0, ±1, ±2...)

Position: ybright = (mλL) / d

YDSE Dark Fringes (Minima)

Occur when the path difference causes destructive interference.

Condition: δ = (m + ½)λ

Position: ydark = (m + ½)λL / d

Fringe Width (β): The distance between any two consecutive bright fringes, or any two consecutive dark fringes. They are evenly spaced.
β = λL / d
📝 Numerical: YDSE Calculation
Problem: In a Young's Double Slit Experiment, the screen is placed 1.5 m away from the slits. The slits are separated by a gap of 0.05 mm. If the third bright fringe is measured to be 4.5 cm from the central maximum, calculate the wavelength of the light used.
Solution:
1. Use the bright fringe position formula: y = (mλL) / d
2. Convert all values to standard SI units (meters):
  • y = 4.5 cm = 0.045 m
  • L = 1.5 m
  • d = 0.05 mm = 5 × 10&supmin;&sup5; m
  • m = 3 (third bright fringe)
3. Rearrange the formula to solve for λ:
λ = (y · d) / (m · L)
4. Substitute the values:
λ = (0.045 × 5 × 10&supmin;&sup5;) / (3 × 1.5)
λ = 2.25 × 10&supmin;&sup6; / 4.5
λ = 5 × 10&supmin;&sup7; m = 500 nm

Fraunhofer Single Slit Diffraction

Diffraction is the bending and spreading of light as it passes around the sharp edges of an obstacle or through a narrow opening. Unlike interference (which uses two distinct slit sources), diffraction in a single slit occurs because of the superposition of infinite secondary wavelets originating from different parts of the same single wavefront passing through the slit.

Fresnel Diffraction

The light source and the viewing screen are at finite, relatively close distances from the obstacle. The wavefronts involved are spherical or cylindrical. The mathematical analysis is highly complex.

Fraunhofer Diffraction (Exam Focus)

The light source and the screen are effectively at an infinite distance (usually achieved practically by using convex lenses). The wavefronts arriving at the slit are perfectly planar (flat). The mathematical analysis is much simpler.

The Single Slit Minima (Dark Fringes)

In a single slit of width 'a', the formula to find the DARK fringes (minima) looks deceptively similar to the formula for the BRIGHT fringes in the double-slit experiment. Do not mix them up on the exam!

Single Slit Dark Fringe Condition: a sin(θ) = mλ
(Where m = ±1, ±2, ±3... but m can never be 0. m=0 represents the central maximum).

Position of Dark Fringe on Screen: ydark = (mλL) / a

Width of the Central Maximum: W = 2λL / a
Feature Interference (YDSE) Diffraction (Single Slit)
Fringe Width All fringes (bright and dark) have exactly equal widths. The central maximum is exactly double the width of all secondary fringes.
Fringe Intensity All bright fringes are equally intense (equally bright). The central maximum contains almost all the light. Intensity falls off rapidly for secondary maxima.
📝 Numerical: Single Slit Central Maximum
Problem: Light of wavelength 500 nm falls perpendicularly on a single slit of width 0.1 mm. The diffraction pattern is observed on a screen placed 2 meters away. Calculate the linear width of the central maximum on the screen.
Solution:
1. Formula for the width of the central maximum: Width = 2λL / a
2. Convert to SI units:
  • λ = 500 nm = 500 × 10&supmin;&sup9; m
  • L = 2 m
  • a = 0.1 mm = 1 × 10&supmin;&sup4; m
3. Calculate:
Width = (2 × 500 × 10&supmin;&sup9; × 2) / (1 × 10&supmin;&sup4;)
Width = (2000 × 10&supmin;&sup9;) / (10&supmin;&sup4;)
Width = 2000 × 10&supmin;&sup5; m = 0.02 m = 2 cm

Unit III: Modern Physics

1. The Timeline of Quantum Mechanics

Modern physics emerged directly from the catastrophic failures of classical Newtonian physics and Maxwell's wave theory to explain atomic and subatomic phenomena at the turn of the 20th century. You must know this timeline for theory questions.

Year Physicist Groundbreaking Contribution
1900 Max Planck Proposed that thermal energy is not emitted in a continuous wave, but is quantized in discrete packets (quanta) with energy E = nhf, solving the Ultraviolet Catastrophe in Blackbody Radiation. This is the birth of Quantum Theory.
1905 Albert Einstein Applied Planck's quantization idea to light itself, proposing that light consists of particle-like "photons." He used this to successfully explain the Photoelectric Effect (winning the Nobel Prize).
1913 Niels Bohr Developed the Bohr Model of the hydrogen atom, showing that electrons orbit the nucleus in discrete, stable, quantized energy levels, and only emit/absorb photons when jumping between them.
1924 Louis de Broglie Proposed Wave-Particle Duality for matter. He hypothesized that if light waves can act like particles, then physical particles (like electrons) must have wave-like properties (λ = h/p).
1925-26 Heisenberg & Schrödinger Formulated the complete, rigorous mathematical framework of modern Quantum Mechanics (Heisenberg's Matrix Mechanics and Schrödinger's Wave Equation).

2. The Photoelectric Effect

The photoelectric effect is the phenomenon where electrons are emitted from the surface of a metal when it is illuminated by light of a sufficiently high frequency. Classical wave theory failed completely to explain the experimental observations. You must know the three main failures:

  1. The Intensity Problem: Classical theory predicted that increasing the brightness (intensity) of the light increases the amplitude of the waves, which should give the ejected electrons more kinetic energy. Reality: Maximum Kinetic Energy is completely independent of light intensity. Turning up the brightness only increases the number of electrons ejected (the photoelectric current), not their speed.
  2. The Threshold Frequency Problem: Classical theory predicted that any frequency of light (even red light) would eventually eject electrons if you shine it bright enough or long enough for energy to build up. Reality: There is a strict cutoff frequency (ν0) for every metal. Below this threshold, absolutely NO emission occurs, no matter how bright the light is or how long you wait.
  3. The Time Delay Problem: Classical theory predicted a noticeable time delay for energy buildup at low light intensities. Reality: Electron emission is virtually instantaneous (less than 10&supmin;&sup9; seconds), even with incredibly dim light.

Einstein's Quantum Solution

Einstein solved this by declaring light is a stream of photons. One photon interacts with exactly one electron. It is an all-or-nothing transfer of energy.

Energy of a single photon: E = hν = hc / λ

The Ultimate Exam Shortcut Formula:
If you know wavelength in nanometers (nm), you can find Energy directly in electron-Volts (eV) without messy scientific notation:
E(eV) = 1240 / λ(nm)

The Work Function (Φ): This is the absolute minimum "tax" or binding energy required to break an electron free from the metal's surface.
Φ = hν0 = hc / λ0 (Where λ0 is the threshold wavelength).

Einstein's Photoelectric Equation: The energy of the incoming photon goes into freeing the electron (Φ), and whatever energy is left over becomes the electron's kinetic energy.

KEmax = Ephoton - Φ
KEmax = hν - hν0
KEmax = (1240 / λ) - Φ

Stopping Potential (V0): In an experimental setup, if you apply a reverse (negative) voltage to the anode, you can repel the incoming electrons. The specific voltage required to stop even the absolute fastest moving photoelectrons from reaching the detector is the Stopping Potential. It is a direct measure of KEmax.

KEmax = e · V0
Therefore: V0 = (h/e)ν - (Φ/e)

3. Graphical Analysis of the Photoelectric Effect

1. Current vs Intensity

Assuming the light is above the threshold frequency, the photoelectric current is directly and linearly proportional to the intensity of light. More intensity = more photons arriving per second = more ejected electrons per second.

2. Current vs Voltage

If you shine light of the same frequency but different intensities, the saturation currents will be different (brighter light = higher plateau), but they will all converge to the exact same Stopping Potential (V0) on the negative x-axis. Intensity does not change KEmax.

3. Stopping Potential (V0) vs Frequency (ν)

This graph forms a straight line. By matching V0 = (h/e)ν - (Φ/e) to y = mx + c:
Slope: h/e. This slope is a universal constant for ALL metals.
X-Intercept: The threshold frequency ν0.
Y-Intercept: -Φ/e.

Notice the pattern: Metals with lower work functions are easier to excite and have longer threshold wavelengths. Cesium is highly sensitive and easily activated by normal visible light, making it the preferred material for photodetectors and night vision equipment. Platinum requires high-energy deep UV light.

📝 Numerical: Comprehensive Photoelectric Calculation
Problem: Light of wavelength 200 nm falls on an aluminum surface (which has a work function Φ = 4.2 eV). Calculate: (a) The energy of the incident photons, (b) The maximum kinetic energy of the emitted electrons, and (c) The stopping potential required.
Solution:
(a) Photon Energy:
Use the 1240 shortcut formula: E = 1240 / λ(nm)
E = 1240 / 200 = 6.2 eV

(b) Maximum Kinetic Energy:
Apply Einstein's equation: KEmax = Ephoton - Φ
KEmax = 6.2 eV - 4.2 eV = 2.0 eV
(Because KE is positive, electrons are successfully emitted).

(c) Stopping Potential (V0):
Since KEmax = eV0, if the kinetic energy is 2.0 electron-Volts, the stopping potential is simply 2.0 Volts.
V0 = 2.0 V
📝 Numerical: Stopping Potential Shift
Problem: In a photoelectric experiment, the stopping potential of a specific metal surface is measured to be 3.0 V when illuminated with light of wavelength 500 nm. Find the new stopping potential V2 if the wavelength is changed to 600 nm.
Solution:
1. Set up the voltage equation for both states. We know that eV = hc/λ - Φ, so V = (1240/λ) - Φ/e.
V1 = (1240 / 500) - Φ/e
V2 = (1240 / 600) - Φ/e

2. Subtract the V2 equation from the V1 equation to eliminate the unknown work function (Φ):
V1 - V2 = 1240 × [ (1 / 500) - (1 / 600) ]
V1 - V2 = 1240 × [ 0.002 - 0.001666... ]
V1 - V2 = 1240 × 0.000333...
V1 - V2 = 0.413 V

3. We know V1 = 3.0 V. Substitute and solve for V2:
3.0 - V2 = 0.413
V2 = 3.0 - 0.413 = 2.587 V (approx 2.59 V)

Unit IV: Electrostatics

1. Electric Charge, Coulomb's Law & Dipoles

Charge is an intrinsic property of matter. It is always conserved and it is quantized: Q = ±ne (where n is an integer, and e = 1.602 × 10&supmin;¹&sup9; C). Like charges repel; opposite charges attract.

Coulomb's Law (Force between point charges):
F = [ 1 / (4πε0) ] · [ |q1q2| / r² ] rˆ
Where ε0 (permittivity of free space) = 8.854 × 10&supmin;¹² C²/(N·m²).
The constant k = 1 / (4πε0) ≈ 9 × 10&sup9; N·m²/C².

Electric Field (E): The force experienced by a unit positive test charge placed in the field: E = F / q0. For a single point charge, the field radiating from it is E = k|q|/r².

Electric Dipoles

An electric dipole consists of two equal and opposite point charges (+q and -q) separated by a small fixed distance 2a. The Electric Dipole Moment (p) is a vector pointing from the negative charge to the positive charge, with magnitude: p = q × (2a).

⚙️ Derivation: Electric Field of a Dipole
1. Field on the Axial Line (End-on position): Consider a point P on the axis of the dipole, at a distance r from the dipole's center. The field from the closer positive charge points away, and the field from the further negative charge points toward the dipole. They oppose each other.
Enet = E+ - E-
Enet = kq / (r - a)² - kq / (r + a)²
Enet = kq [ ((r + a)² - (r - a)²) / (r² - a²)² ]
Enet = kq [ 4ra / (r² - a²)² ] = k(q · 2a) · 2r / (r² - a²)² = k(p) · 2r / (r² - a²)²
For a short dipole where r >> a, we can ignore a in the denominator (r² - a² ≈ r²).
Final: Eaxial ≈ 2kp / r³ (Note that dipole fields drop off as 1/r³, much faster than point charges).
2. Field on the Equatorial Line (Broadside position): Consider point P on the perpendicular bisector, distance r from the center. The distance from P to either charge is √(r²+a²). The vertical components of E+ and E- cancel perfectly. The horizontal components (parallel to the dipole) add up.
Enet = 2E cos(θ)
We know E = kq / (r² + a²). From geometry, cos(θ) = a / √(r² + a²).
Enet = 2 · [ kq / (r² + a²) ] · [ a / (r² + a²)1/2 ]
Enet = k(q · 2a) / (r² + a²)3/2 = kp / (r² + a²)3/2
For a short dipole where r >> a:
Final: Eequatorial ≈ kp / r³

When placed in a uniform external electric field, a dipole experiences a twisting force.
Torque on Dipole: τ = p × E = pE sin(θ).
Potential Energy of Dipole: U = -p · E = -pE cos(θ).

2. Gauss's Law & Area Integrals

Electric Flux (ΦE) measures the total number of electric field lines passing through a given surface area. Mathematically, it is the surface integral of the dot product of the Electric Field vector and the Area normal vector: ΦE = ∫ E · dA.

Gauss's Law Statement:
The net electric flux through any closed, hypothetical 3D surface (a Gaussian surface) is exactly equal to the net charge enclosed within that surface divided by the permittivity of free space.
∮ E · dA = Qenclosed / ε0
⚙️ Derivation 1: Electric Field of an Infinite Line of Charge

Consider an infinitely long straight wire with a uniform linear charge density λ (Charge per unit length).

Step 1: Choose Gaussian Surface By symmetry, the electric field points radially outward from the wire. We choose a cylinder of radius 'r' and length 'l' coaxial with the wire.
Step 2: Calculate Flux (∮ E · dA) The cylinder has 3 parts: Top flat cap, bottom flat cap, and curved outer surface.
- Top & Bottom caps: The Area vector points vertically, while E points radially outward. The angle is 90°. Since cos(90°)=0, Flux = 0.
- Curved surface: E is perfectly parallel to dA (Angle = 0°, cos(0)=1). The area of the curved surface is 2πrl.
Total Flux ΦE = E(2πrl).
Step 3: Calculate Enclosed Charge The length of wire physically enclosed by our imaginary cylinder is 'l'.
Qencl = λ · l
Step 4: Apply Gauss's Law ΦE = Qencl / ε0
E(2πrl) = (λl) / ε0
Final Result: Cancel 'l' from both sides to get:
E = λ / (2πε0r)
⚙️ Derivation 2: Electric Field of an Infinite Plane Sheet

Consider an infinite, thin, non-conducting sheet with uniform surface charge density σ (Charge per unit area).

Step 1: Choose Gaussian Surface We choose a cylindrical "pillbox" piercing straight through the sheet. Let the cross-sectional area of the flat end caps be 'A'.
Step 2: Calculate Flux - Curved surface: The E field is perpendicular to the area vector here. Flux = 0.
- Two flat caps: The E field points straight outward, parallel to the dA vectors on both sides. Flux through one cap = EA. Since there are two caps (front and back), Total Flux ΦE = 2EA.
Step 3: Calculate Enclosed Charge The pillbox cuts a circle of area A out of the charged sheet.
Qencl = σ · A
Step 4: Apply Gauss's Law 2EA = (σA) / ε0
Final Result: Cancel 'A' to get:
E = σ / (2ε0)
Crucial Note: The electric field produced by an infinite sheet is completely uniform. It does not weaken with distance 'r' away from the sheet!
⚙️ Derivation 3: Gauss's Law for Spheres

Consider a sphere of radius R carrying a total charge Q. We want to find the E-field at a distance 'r' from the center. The Gaussian surface is a concentric sphere of area 4πr². So the flux is always E(4πr²). The only thing that changes is the enclosed charge.

Case A: Conducting Spherical Shell In any conductor, excess charge violently repels itself and distributes entirely on the outer surface. The interior is empty of net charge.
- Inside the shell (r < R): Qencl = 0. Therefore, E(4πr²) = 0 ⇒ E = 0. (This is the principle of electrostatic shielding/Faraday cages).
- Outside the shell (r ≥ R): The Gaussian sphere is larger than the shell, so it encloses all the charge. Qencl = Q.
E(4πr²) = Q / ε0 ⇒ E = Q / (4πε0r²) = kQ/r². (It acts exactly like a point charge concentrated at the center).
Case B: Solid Non-Conducting Sphere Imagine an insulating plastic sphere where the charge is frozen uniformly throughout its entire volume. The volume charge density is ρ = Q / (4/3 π R³).
- Inside the sphere (r < R): Our smaller Gaussian sphere of radius 'r' only encloses a fraction of the total charge.
Qencl = ρ × Vgaussian = [ Q / (4/3 π R³) ] × [ 4/3 π r³ ] = Q(r³ / R³).
Apply Gauss's Law: E(4πr²) = Q(r³ / R³) / ε0.
Cancel r²: E = Qr / (4πε0R³) = kQr/R³. (Inside an insulator, the E-field increases linearly from zero at the center to a maximum at the surface).
- Outside the sphere (r ≥ R): Same as the conducting shell. It encloses all the charge, so it acts like a point charge: E = kQ/r².

3. Electric Potential & Capacitance

Electric Potential (V) is the potential energy per unit charge: V = U / q0. The unit is Volts (Joules/Coulomb). For a single point charge, V = kq/r.

A capacitor is a device designed to store electrical potential energy by separating opposite charges. Capacitance (C) measures how much charge it can store per unit of voltage applied: C = Q / V. Measured in Farads (F).

⚙️ Derivation: Capacitance of a Parallel Plate Capacitor

Consider two parallel conducting plates, each of Area A, separated by a small gap distance d. We place charge +Q on one plate and -Q on the other. Surface charge density σ = Q/A.

Step 1: Electric Field between the plates Each plate acts like an infinite sheet of charge. The positive plate pushes the field away (+σ/2ε0) and the negative plate pulls the field toward itself (+σ/2ε0). In the gap between them, the fields point in the exact same direction and add up perfectly.
E = σ/(2ε0) + σ/(2ε0) = σ / ε0.
Substituting σ = Q/A gives: E = Q / (ε0A).
Step 2: Voltage (Potential Difference) Since the electric field between flat parallel plates is completely uniform, the voltage is simply the field strength multiplied by the distance across the gap.
V = E · d.
Substitute E from Step 1: V = (Q · d) / (ε0A).
Step 3: Calculate Capacitance By definition, C = Q / V.
C = Q / [ (Q · d) / (ε0A) ]
Final Result: The charge Q cancels out, proving that capacitance depends only on physical geometry, not on how much charge is present.
C = ε0A / d

Advanced Capacitance Topics

Energy Stored in a Capacitor:
U = ½CV² = Q² / (2C) = ½QV

Combinations of Capacitors:
Series: Capacitors are placed in a single line. They all share the exact same Charge (Q), but the total Voltage splits among them.
1/Ceq = 1/C1 + 1/C2 + 1/C3 ...
Parallel: Capacitors are placed on separate branches. They all share the exact same Voltage (V) across their terminals, but the total Charge splits.
Ceq = C1 + C2 + C3 ...

Dielectrics:
If you insert an insulating material (a dielectric, like glass or plastic) with a dielectric constant 'κ' completely between the plates of a capacitor, it polarizes and weakens the internal electric field. This allows the plates to store more charge for the same voltage.
The new capacitance is always higher: Cnew = κCoriginal.
📝 Numerical: Parallel-Plate Capacitor Calculation
Problem: The plates of a parallel-plate capacitor placed in a vacuum are 5.00 mm apart and have a surface area of 2.00 m². A 10.0-kV potential difference is applied across the capacitor by a power supply. Compute (a) the capacitance of the device and (b) the total charge accumulated on each plate.
Given Parameters:
d = 5 mm = 5 × 10&supmin;³ m
A = 2.00 m²
V = 10.0 kV = 10,000 V
ε0 = 8.85 × 10&supmin;¹² F/m

(a) Calculating Capacitance:
C = ε0A / d
C = (8.85 × 10&supmin;¹² × 2) / (5 × 10&supmin;³)
C = 17.7 × 10&supmin;¹² / 0.005
C = 3.54 × 10&supmin;&sup9; F = 3.54 nF (nanoFarads)

(b) Calculating Charge:
Q = C × V
Q = (3.54 × 10&supmin;&sup9; F) × (10 × 10³ V)
Q = 35.4 × 10&supmin;&sup6; C = 35.4 μC (microCoulombs)

Unit V: Magnetostatics

1. Magnetic Fields, Forces & Dipoles

Unlike electric charges, which can exist as isolated positive or negative points, magnetic monopoles do not exist in nature. If you break a bar magnet in half, you do not get an isolated North pole and an isolated South pole; you instantly get two smaller, complete dipoles. Because of this, magnetic field lines have no true beginning or end—they always form closed continuous loops. They point from North to South outside a magnet, and South to North inside.

The Lorentz Force

A stationary electric charge experiences absolutely no magnetic force. A charge 'q' must be moving with a velocity 'v' through a magnetic field 'B' to experience a force. The direction of this force is given by the right-hand rule for the cross product.

Magnetic Force Vector: FB = q (v × B)
Magnitude: FB = qvB sin(θ) (where θ is the angle between velocity and B-field).

Total Lorentz Force (When both Electric and Magnetic fields are present):
Ftotal = q(E + v × B)
⚙️ Derivation: Radius of a Charged Particle in a Magnetic Field

If a charged particle enters a uniform magnetic field perfectly perpendicular to its velocity (θ = 90°), the magnetic force acts perpendicular to the velocity at all times. By definition, a force perpendicular to velocity does no work, but it acts as a centripetal force, forcing the particle into uniform circular motion.

Step 1: Equate the Physical Forces Magnetic Force = Centripetal Force
qvB sin(90°) = m(v² / r)
Step 2: Solve for the Radius (r) Since sin(90°) = 1:
qvB = mv² / r
Cancel one 'v' from both sides: qB = mv / r
Final Result: Multiply by r and divide by qB:
r = mv / (qB)

Physical Observation: Faster or heavier particles (high momentum 'mv') swing wide and have larger radii. Stronger magnetic fields or highly charged particles get pulled into tighter, smaller circles.

Magnetic Forces on Current-Carrying Conductors

Since current is just a flow of moving charges, a macroscopic wire carrying current also experiences a magnetic force. For a straight wire of length L carrying current I in a uniform B-field:

F = I (L × B)

⚙️ Derivation: Force Between Two Parallel Wires

Consider two infinitely long, straight, parallel wires carrying currents I1 and I2, separated by a distance d. How do they affect each other?

Step 1: Wire 1 creates a magnetic field Wire 1 generates a magnetic field that extends outward. At the exact location of Wire 2 (distance d away), the strength of this field is:
B1 = μ0I1 / (2πd)
Step 2: Wire 2 experiences a force Wire 2 sits in the magnetic field B1. The force on a length L of Wire 2 is:
Fon 2 = I2 L B1 (Assuming wires are parallel, so the angle with the field loops is 90°).
Substitute B1 into the equation:
Fon 2 = I2 L [ μ0I1 / (2πd) ]
Final Result: Force per unit length Since the wires are infinite, we define the force per meter of wire:
F / L = μ0 I1 I2 / (2πd)

Right Hand Rule Consequences: If the currents flow in the same direction, the wires attract each other. If they flow in opposite directions, they repel.

Magnetic Dipoles

A simple loop of wire carrying a current acts exactly like a tiny bar magnet. It has a North face and a South face.

Magnetic Dipole Moment (μ): For a coil with N turns, cross-sectional area A, carrying current I.
μ = N I A (Vector points normal to the loop area via Right-Hand Rule).

Torque on a Loop in a B-field: τ = μ × B.

2. Sources of Magnetic Field: Biot-Savart Law

The Biot-Savart Law allows us to calculate the infinitesimal magnetic field dB produced by a tiny snippet of wire carrying current (a current element Idl). It is the magnetic equivalent of Coulomb's Law.
dB = (μ0 I / 4π) · (dl × rˆ) / r²

⚙️ Derivation: Magnetic Field of a Circular Current Loop

Consider a circular loop of wire of radius R carrying a steady current I.

1. At the Exact Center of the Loop:
We are finding B at the center point. Every tiny segment dl of the ring is at a distance r = R from the center. The angle between the current element dl (tangent to the ring) and the position vector r (pointing to center) is exactly 90°. sin(90°) = 1.
Magnitude of dB = (μ0I / 4πR²) dl.
To find the total field, we integrate around the entire loop. The integral of dl around a circle is its circumference, 2πR.
B = (μ0I / 4πR²) × (2πR)
Result: Bcenter = μ0I / (2R)
2. On the Central Axis (distance x from the center):
We are finding B at point P, which is a distance x away from the center along the axis. The distance from any segment dl on the ring to point P forms a right triangle: r = √(R² + x²).
Due to the cylindrical symmetry of the ring, all the magnetic field components perpendicular to the axis perfectly cancel each other out. Only the horizontal axial components (dBx = dB cosθ) survive and add up.
From the geometry of the setup, cosθ = R / r = R / √(R² + x²).
dBaxial = (μ0I dl / 4πr²) · (R/r) = (μ0I R / 4πr³) dl
Integrate dl around the loop (2πR):
B = (μ0I R / 4πr³) × 2πR = (μ0I R²) / (2r³)
Substitute r back in:
Result: Baxis = μ0 I R² / [ 2(R² + x²)3/2 ]

3. Ampere's Law: Solenoids & Toroids

Ampere's Law is the magnetic equivalent of Gauss's Law. It is highly useful for calculating magnetic fields for highly symmetric current distributions (like infinite straight wires and coils).

Ampere's Law Statement:
The line integral of the magnetic field around any closed hypothetical loop (an Amperian loop) is equal to μ0 times the net steady current punching through the area enclosed by that loop.
∮ B · dl = μ0 Ienclosed
Where μ0 (permeability of free space) = 4π × 10&supmin;&sup7; T·m/A
⚙️ Derivation 1: Magnetic Field of an Infinite Straight Wire

Consider an infinitely long straight wire carrying a steady current I. We want to find the magnetic field B at a radial distance 'r'.

Step 1: Choose an Amperian Loop By symmetry (Right-Hand Rule), the magnetic field lines form concentric circles around the wire. We choose a circular loop of radius 'r' centered perfectly on the wire.
Step 2: Evaluate the Line Integral At every point on our circular loop, the B-field vector is tangential to the circle, and our path element dl is also tangential. Therefore, they are parallel (angle θ = 0°, cos(0) = 1).
∮ B · dl = ∮ B dl cos(0°) = B ∮ dl
The integral of dl around a complete circle is its circumference (2πr).
∮ B · dl = B(2πr)
Step 3: Apply Ampere's Law B(2πr) = μ0 Ienclosed
Since the loop encircles the entire wire, it encloses all the current. Iencl = I.
Final Result: B = μ0I / (2πr)
⚙️ Derivation 2: Magnetic Field Inside an Ideal Solenoid

A solenoid is a long coil of wire. For an "ideal" solenoid (infinitely long, with tightly packed turns), the magnetic field is strong, uniform, and parallel to the axis inside, and practically zero everywhere outside.
Let the solenoid have 'N' total turns over a length 'L'. Let 'n' be the number of turns per unit length (n = N/L). The current is 'I'.

Step 1: Choose an Amperian Loop We choose a rectangular loop (sides 1-2-3-4) of length 'l' and width 'w'. Side 1 is perfectly parallel to the central axis inside the solenoid. Side 3 is outside the solenoid. Sides 2 and 4 cut vertically across the coils.
Step 2: Evaluate the Line Integral in four parts ∮ B · dl = ∫1 B·dl + ∫2 B·dl + ∫3 B·dl + ∫4 B·dl

- Side 1 (Inside): B and dl are parallel. Integral = B · l
- Sides 2 & 4 (Crossing): The B-field is horizontal, but dl is vertical. They are perpendicular (dot product = 0). Integral = 0
- Side 3 (Outside): B ≈ 0 outside an ideal solenoid. Integral = 0

Summing them up: ∮ B · dl = Bl + 0 + 0 + 0 = Bl
Step 3: Calculate Enclosed Current The rectangular loop frames a certain section of the coil. Since there are 'n' turns per unit length, a length 'l' encloses exactly (n × l) turns of wire. Each turn carries current I.
Iencl = (n × l) × I
Step 4: Apply Ampere's Law Bl = μ0 (n · l · I)
Final Result: Cancel the length 'l' from both sides to get:
B = μ0 n I
(Note: Because 'l' and 'w' cancelled out, the magnetic field is perfectly uniform everywhere inside the solenoid, independent of radius or position!)
⚙️ Derivation 3: The Toroid

A toroid is essentially a solenoid bent into a seamless doughnut shape. Let it have an inner radius r1, an outer radius r2, and a total of N turns of wire carrying current I.

Step 1: Choose an Amperian Loop Because of the circular symmetry, the magnetic field lines form closed circles entirely contained within the core of the doughnut. We choose a circular Amperian loop of radius r located entirely inside the toroid core (so r1 < r < r2).
Step 2: Evaluate the Line Integral & Enclosed Current Just like the straight wire, B and dl are tangential and parallel everywhere on the circle.
∮ B dl = B(2πr).
What current pierces the "hole" of our circular loop? The loop encircles the inner hole of the doughnut. Every single one of the N turns of wire comes up through that hole. Therefore, the total enclosed current is N times I.
Iencl = NI.
Final Result: Apply Ampere's Law B(2πr) = μ0(NI)
B = μ0NI / (2πr)
(Unlike the straight solenoid, the magnetic field inside a toroid is NOT uniform. Because of the 'r' in the denominator, the magnetic field is slightly stronger near the inner edge than near the outer edge).
