1. Vector Analysis

Vector Fundamentals & Products

A scalar is a physical quantity with magnitude only (mass, time). A vector possesses both magnitude and direction (force, velocity). Vectors are represented in 3D Cartesian coordinates using unit vectors iˆ, jˆ, and kˆ.

General Vector: A = Axiˆ + Ayjˆ + Azkˆ
Magnitude: |A| = √(Ax² + Ay² + Az²)
Unit Vector (Direction): Aˆ = A / |A|

Dot Product (Scalar Product)

The result of a dot product is a scalar number. It tells us how much of one vector goes in the direction of another.

A · B = |A||B| cos(θ)

A · B = AxBx + AyBy + AzBz

Orthogonality Principle: If two vectors are perpendicular to each other (θ = 90°), their dot product is strictly zero: A · B = 0.

Cross Product (Vector Product)

The result of a cross product is a new vector that is perfectly perpendicular to the plane formed by the original two vectors.

A × B = |A||B| sin(θ) nˆ

Parallel Principle: If two vectors are parallel or anti-parallel (θ = 0° or 180°), their cross product is zero: A × B = 0.

📝 Numerical: Orthogonal Vectors
Problem: Given vectors A = 2iˆ - 3jˆ + 7kˆ and B = 5iˆ + 1jˆ + Xkˆ. Find X if vectors A and B are orthogonal.
Step-by-Step Solution:
1. Condition for orthogonality: A · B = 0
2. Apply dot product: (2)(5) + (-3)(1) + (7)(X) = 0
3. Simplify: 10 - 3 + 7X = 0
4. Solve for X: 7 + 7X = 0 ⇒ 7X = -7 ⇒ X = -1
2. Forces & Mechanics

Newton's Laws & Friction Dynamics

Newton's First Law: A body remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by a net external force.
Newton's Second Law: The rate of change of linear momentum is directly proportional to the applied force: ΣF = ma.
Newton's Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

Friction

Friction is a non-conservative contact force that always opposes relative motion between two surfaces.

  • Static Friction (fs): Adjusts itself to prevent motion. It has a maximum limit called limiting friction: fs ≤ μsN
  • Kinetic Friction (fk): Constant force acting when the object is actively sliding: fk = μkN (Note: μk < μs)
⚙️ Derivation: Acceleration of a Block on a Rough Inclined Plane

Consider a block of mass 'm' sliding down a rough inclined plane making an angle θ with the horizontal. Coefficient of kinetic friction is μk.

Step 1: Resolve gravitational force (mg)
Downward along the incline: Fparallel = mg sin(θ)
Perpendicular into the incline: Fperpendicular = mg cos(θ)
Step 2: Determine Normal Force (N)
Since there is no vertical bouncing off the plane, N balances the perpendicular component:
N = mg cos(θ)
Step 3: Define Kinetic Friction (fk)
Friction acts up the incline to oppose the downward sliding motion:
fk = μkN = μk(mg cosθ)
Step 4: Apply Newton's Second Law along the plane
ΣF = ma
(Force pulling down) - (Friction pulling up) = ma
mg sin(θ) - μkmg cos(θ) = ma
Step 5: Final Acceleration Formula
Cancel 'm' from all terms:
a = g [ sin(θ) - μkcos(θ) ]

*Concept - Angle of Repose (θc): The maximum angle before sliding starts. Here, a = 0, so mg sin(θc) = μsmg cos(θc). Therefore, tan(θc) = μs.

3. Momentum, Work & Energy

Conservation Laws and Theorems

Linear Momentum & Impulse

Linear momentum (P) is defined as P = mv. By Newton's second law, F = dP/dt. If the net external force is zero, momentum is conserved (Pinitial = Pfinal).

Impulse (I): The total effect of a large force acting over a short time (like a bat hitting a ball). It is the area under the Force-Time curve.

I = ∫ F dt = Favg × Δt
Impulse-Momentum Theorem: I = ΔP = Pfinal - Pinitial

Work & The Work-Energy Theorem

Work is defined as the dot product of Force and Displacement: W = ∫ F · ds.

⚙️ Derivation: The Work-Energy Theorem

Statement: The net work done by all forces on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy.

Step 1: Start with the definition of Work
W = ∫ F dx
Step 2: Apply Newton's Second Law
F = ma = m(dv/dt)
Step 3: Chain Rule for Acceleration
a = (dv/dx) × (dx/dt) = v(dv/dx)
Therefore, F = m [ v(dv/dx) ]
Step 4: Substitute into the Work integral
W = ∫ [m v (dv/dx)] dx = ∫ m v dv
Step 5: Integrate from initial velocity (u) to final velocity (v)
W = m [ v² / 2 ] evaluated from u to v
W = ½mv² - ½mu²
Final Result:
Wnet = KEfinal - KEinitial = ΔKE
📝 Numerical: The Ballistic Pendulum
Problem: A bullet of mass m1 and velocity vA is fired into a large wooden block of mass m2 suspended by strings. The bullet embeds in the block, and the entire system swings up to a maximum height h. Derive the formula to find the initial speed of the bullet vA.
Methodology (Two-Part Physics Problem):
Part 1: The Collision (Conservation of Momentum)
Right before collision, only the bullet moves. Right after, they move together with velocity vB.
m1vA + m2(0) = (m1 + m2)vB
Equation 1: vB = (m1vA) / (m1 + m2)

Part 2: The Swing (Conservation of Mechanical Energy)
After the collision, the block+bullet system swings upward. Kinetic energy at the bottom converts entirely to Potential energy at height h.
KEbottom = PEtop
½(m1 + m2)vB² = (m1 + m2)gh
Cancel masses: ½vB² = gh ⇒ vB = √(2gh)

Part 3: Substitute to find vA
Substitute vB back into Equation 1:
√(2gh) = (m1vA) / (m1 + m2)
Final Formula: vA = [ (m1 + m2) / m1 ] × √(2gh)
1. Wave Fundamentals

Wave Types and The EM Spectrum

Mechanical vs Electromagnetic: Mechanical waves require a physical medium to propagate (like sound). Electromagnetic (EM) waves consist of oscillating electric and magnetic fields and can travel through a vacuum at the speed of light (c = 3 × 10&sup8; m/s).

Transverse Waves

The particles of the medium oscillate perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation. Contains crests and troughs.

Examples: Light waves, EM waves, ripples on water.

Longitudinal Waves

The particles of the medium oscillate parallel to the direction of wave propagation. Contains compressions and rarefactions.

Examples: Sound waves, spring oscillations.

The Electromagnetic Spectrum

Ordered by increasing wavelength (and therefore decreasing frequency and decreasing energy):

Gamma Rays → X-Rays → Ultraviolet (UV) → Visible Light (400nm - 750nm) → Infrared (IR) → Microwaves → Radio Waves
2. Interference

Young's Double Slit Experiment (YDSE)

Interference is the phenomenon where two waves superimpose to form a resultant wave of greater, lower, or the same amplitude. Strict Conditions: The two sources must be coherent (maintaining a constant phase difference) and monochromatic (single wavelength).

⚙️ Derivation: Path Difference in YDSE

Consider two slits S1 and S2 separated by distance 'd'. A screen is placed at distance 'L' (where L >> d). A point P on the screen is at distance 'y' from the central axis.

Step 1: Geometric Path Difference (δ)
The wave from S2 travels further than S1 to reach P. If we drop a perpendicular from S1 to S2P, the extra distance is δ = S2P - S1P.
Step 2: Small Angle Approximation
Because L is very large, the rays are nearly parallel. The angle θ made by the point P from the center of the slits gives:
δ ≈ d sin(θ)
Step 3: Relate to Screen Position (y)
For small angles, sin(θ) ≈ tan(θ) = y / L.
Therefore, Path Difference δ = (y · d) / L

Conditions for Fringes

Type of Fringe Path Difference Condition Position on Screen (y)
Constructive (Bright) δ = mλ (where m = 0, ±1, ±2...) y = (m · λ · L) / d
Destructive (Dark) δ = (m + ½)λ y = (m + ½) · λ · L / d
Fringe Width (β): The distance between any two consecutive bright or dark fringes.
β = λL / d
3. Diffraction

Fraunhofer Single Slit Diffraction

Diffraction is the bending of light around the corners of an obstacle. Unlike interference (two distinct sources), diffraction in a single slit occurs due to the interference of secondary wavelets originating from different parts of the same wavefront.

Fresnel Diffraction

Source and screen are at finite distances from the obstacle. Wavefronts are spherical or cylindrical. Math is complex.

Fraunhofer Diffraction (Exam Focus)

Source and screen are effectively at infinity (achieved using convex lenses). Wavefronts are planar. Math is simpler.

The Single Slit Minima (Dark Fringes)

In a single slit of width 'a', the condition for destructive interference is surprisingly similar to the constructive condition for YDSE. Do not confuse them!

Dark Fringe Condition: a sin(θ) = mλ   (where m = ±1, ±2, ±3... but never 0)
Width of Central Maximum: W = 2λL / a

Note: The central fringe (m=0) is the brightest and widest. Its width is exactly double the width of all the secondary maxima.

1. The Photoelectric Effect

Failures of Classical Wave Theory

When light hits a metal surface, electrons can be ejected. The classical wave theory of light completely failed to explain the experimental observations of this effect:

  • Classical Prediction: Higher intensity light means waves with more energy, which should give ejected electrons more kinetic energy.
    Reality: Maximum Kinetic Energy is completely independent of light intensity. Intensity only changes the number of electrons ejected (the current).
  • Classical Prediction: Any frequency of light should eventually eject electrons if you shine it long enough for energy to build up.
    Reality: Emission is instantaneous (< 10&supmin;&sup9; s). Furthermore, there is a strict Threshold Frequency (ν0). Below this frequency, no electrons are ejected, no matter how bright the light is or how long it shines.
  • Classical Prediction: Frequency shouldn't affect electron kinetic energy.
    Reality: Maximum Kinetic Energy increases linearly with the frequency of incident light.
2. Einstein's Quantum Theory

Photons and the Photoelectric Equation

Albert Einstein solved the problem in 1905 (winning the Nobel Prize) by proposing that light behaves as a stream of quantized particles called photons. One photon interacts with exactly one electron.

Energy of a single photon: E = hν = hc / λ
The Ultimate Exam Shortcut: E(eV) = 1240 / λ(nm)

The Work Function (Φ)

The absolute minimum energy required to break an electron free from the metal's surface.
Φ = hν0 = hc / λ0

Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

The energy of the incoming photon goes into freeing the electron (Φ), and whatever energy is left over becomes the electron's kinetic energy.

KEmax = Ephoton - Φ
KEmax = hν - hν0

Stopping Potential (V0)

The negative voltage required to repel and stop even the fastest moving photoelectrons from reaching the anode. It directly measures KEmax.

KEmax = e · V0
Therefore, V0 = (h/e)ν - (Φ/e)
3. Graphical Analysis

Stopping Potential vs Frequency Graph

If you plot Stopping Potential (V0) on the Y-axis and Frequency (ν) on the X-axis, the equation V0 = (h/e)ν - (Φ/e) represents a straight line (y = mx + c).

Graph Feature Physical Meaning Significance
Slope (m) h / e The slope is constant for ALL metals. You can calculate Planck's constant by multiplying the slope by the charge of an electron (e).
X-intercept ν0 The threshold frequency of that specific metal.
Y-intercept -Φ / e Related to the work function of the metal.
📝 Numerical: Comprehensive Photoelectric Calculation
Problem: A Sodium (Na) surface with a work function of Φ = 2.46 eV is illuminated with ultraviolet light of wavelength 300 nm. Find: (a) Photon Energy, (b) Max Kinetic Energy of electrons, (c) Threshold Wavelength.
Step-by-Step Solution:
(a) Photon Energy (using shortcut)
E = 1240 / λ(nm) = 1240 / 300 = 4.133 eV

(b) Maximum Kinetic Energy
KEmax = E - Φ = 4.133 eV - 2.46 eV = 1.673 eV
(Note: Since KE > 0, emission does occur. The stopping potential would be exactly 1.673 Volts).

(c) Threshold Wavelength (λ0)
λ0 = 1240 / Φ(eV) = 1240 / 2.46 = 504 nm
(Note: 504nm is green light. Sodium will emit electrons for any light with a wavelength shorter than 504nm, like blue, violet, or UV).
1. Fundamentals

Electric Charge & Coulomb's Law

Charge is quantized: Q = ±ne (where n is an integer, and e = 1.602 × 10&supmin;¹&sup9; C). Like charges repel; opposite charges attract.

Coulomb's Law (Force between point charges):
F = [ 1 / (4πε0) ] · [ |q1q2| / r² ] rˆ
Where ε0 (permittivity of free space) = 8.854 × 10&supmin;¹² C²/(N·m²).
The constant k = 1 / (4πε0) ≈ 9 × 10&sup9; N·m²/C².

Electric Field (E)

The force experienced by a unit positive test charge placed in the field: E = F / q0. For a point charge, E = k|q|/r².

2. Gauss's Law

Electric Flux and Theorem

Electric Flux (ΦE) measures the number of electric field lines passing through a given surface area. Mathematically, it is the dot product of the Electric Field vector and the Area vector: ΦE = ∫ E · dA.

Gauss's Law Statement:
The net electric flux through any closed surface (Gaussian surface) is equal to the net charge enclosed divided by the permittivity of free space.
∮ E · dA = Qenclosed / ε0
⚙️ Derivation 1: Electric Field of an Infinite Line of Charge

Consider an infinitely long straight wire with a uniform linear charge density λ (Charge per unit length).

Step 1: Choose Gaussian Surface
By symmetry, the electric field points radially outward. We choose a cylinder of radius 'r' and length 'l' coaxial with the wire.
Step 2: Calculate Flux (∮ E · dA)
The cylinder has 3 parts: Top flat cap, bottom flat cap, and curved surface.
- Top & Bottom caps: The Area vector points vertically, while E points radially outward. Angle is 90°. Since cos(90°)=0, Flux = 0.
- Curved surface: E is parallel to dA (Angle = 0°, cos(0)=1). Area of curved surface is 2πrl.
Total Flux ΦE = E(2πrl).
Step 3: Calculate Enclosed Charge
The length of wire enclosed by our cylinder is 'l'.
Qencl = λ · l
Step 4: Apply Gauss's Law
E(2πrl) = (λl) / ε0
Final Result:
Cancel 'l' to get: E = λ / (2πε0r)
⚙️ Derivation 2: Electric Field of an Infinite Plane Sheet

Consider an infinite, thin, non-conducting sheet with uniform surface charge density σ (Charge per unit area).

Step 1: Choose Gaussian Surface
We choose a cylindrical "pillbox" piercing through the sheet. Let the cross-sectional area of the flat caps be 'A'.
Step 2: Calculate Flux
- Curved surface: E field is perpendicular to the area vector. Flux = 0.
- Two flat caps: E field points outward, parallel to dA. Flux through one cap = EA. Since there are two caps (front and back), Total Flux ΦE = 2EA.
Step 3: Calculate Enclosed Charge
The pillbox cuts a circle of area A out of the sheet.
Qencl = σ · A
Step 4: Apply Gauss's Law
2EA = (σA) / ε0
Final Result:
Cancel 'A' to get: E = σ / (2ε0)
Note: The electric field is uniform and does not depend on distance 'r'!
3. Capacitors

Potential & Parallel Plate Capacitance

A capacitor stores electrical potential energy. Capacitance (C) measures how much charge it can store per unit of voltage: C = Q / V. Measured in Farads (F).

⚙️ Derivation: Capacitance of a Parallel Plate Capacitor

Consider two parallel conducting plates of Area A, separated by distance d. One plate has charge +Q, the other -Q. Surface charge density σ = Q/A.

Step 1: Electric Field between plates
Between two oppositely charged plates, the fields add up: E = σ/(2ε0) + σ/(2ε0) = σ / ε0.
Substituting σ = Q/A gives: E = Q / (ε0A).
Step 2: Potential Difference (V)
Since the field is uniform, Voltage is Field times distance: V = E · d.
Substituting E: V = (Q · d) / (ε0A).
Step 3: Calculate Capacitance
C = Q / V
C = Q / [ (Q · d) / (ε0A) ]
Final Result:
Cancel Q to get: C = ε0A / d

Energy Stored: U = ½CV² = Q² / (2C)

📝 Numerical: Parallel-Plate Capacitor Calculation
Problem: The plates of a parallel-plate capacitor in a vacuum are 5.00 mm apart and have an area of 2.00 m². A 10.0-kV potential difference is applied across the capacitor. Compute (a) the capacitance and (b) the charge on each plate.
Given: d = 5 × 10&supmin;³ m, A = 2.00 m², V = 10,000 V.

(a) Capacitance:
C = ε0A / d
C = (8.85 × 10&supmin;¹² × 2) / (5 × 10&supmin;³)
C = 17.7 × 10&supmin;¹² / 0.005 = 3.54 × 10&supmin;&sup9; F = 3.54 nF

(b) Charge:
Q = C × V
Q = (3.54 × 10&supmin;&sup9;) × (10 × 10³)
Q = 35.4 × 10&supmin;&sup6; C = 35.4 μC
1. Magnetic Fundamentals

Magnetic Fields & Forces

Unlike electric charges, magnetic monopoles do not exist. If you break a bar magnet in half, you get two smaller dipoles, each with a North and South pole. Magnetic field lines always form closed continuous loops, pointing from North to South externally, and South to North internally.

Lorentz Force on a Moving Charge

A stationary charge experiences no magnetic force. A charge 'q' moving with velocity 'v' in a magnetic field 'B' experiences a force given by the cross product:

FB = q (v × B)
Magnitude: FB = qvB sin(θ)
Total Lorentz Force (Electric + Magnetic): F = q(E + v × B)
⚙️ Derivation: Radius of a Charged Particle in a Magnetic Field

If a charged particle enters a uniform magnetic field perpendicular to its velocity (θ = 90°), the magnetic force is perpendicular to the velocity, acting as a centripetal force causing uniform circular motion.

Step 1: Equate Forces
Magnetic Force = Centripetal Force
qvB sin(90°) = m(v² / r)
Step 2: Solve for Radius (r)
qvB = mv² / r
Cancel one 'v': qB = mv / r
Final Result:
r = mv / (qB)

Observation: Faster or heavier particles have larger radii. Stronger fields or higher charges cause tighter (smaller) radii.

2. Sources of Magnetic Field

Biot-Savart & Ampere's Law

The Biot-Savart Law gives the infinitesimal magnetic field dB produced by a small current element Idl. It is the magnetic equivalent of Coulomb's Law.
dB = (μ0 I / 4π) · (dl × rˆ) / r²

Ampere's Law Statement:
The line integral of the magnetic field around any closed loop is equal to μ0 times the net steady current enclosed by the loop. It is the magnetic equivalent of Gauss's Law, highly useful for symmetric current distributions.
∮ B · dl = μ0 Ienclosed
Where μ0 (permeability of free space) = 4π × 10&supmin;&sup7; T·m/A
⚙️ Derivation 1: Magnetic Field of an Infinite Straight Wire

Consider an infinitely long straight wire carrying a steady current I. We want to find the magnetic field B at a radial distance 'r'.

Step 1: Choose Amperian Loop
By symmetry, the magnetic field lines form concentric circles around the wire (Right-Hand Rule). We choose a circular loop of radius 'r' centered on the wire.
Step 2: Evaluate the Line Integral
At every point on the circular loop, B is tangential, and the path element dl is also tangential. Therefore, angle θ = 0°, cos(0) = 1.
∮ B · dl = ∮ B dl = B ∮ dl
The integral of dl around a circle is its circumference (2πr).
∮ B · dl = B(2πr)
Step 3: Apply Ampere's Law
B(2πr) = μ0 Ienclosed
Since the loop encloses the entire wire, Iencl = I.
Final Result:
B = μ0I / (2πr)
⚙️ Derivation 2: Magnetic Field Inside an Ideal Solenoid

A solenoid is a long coil of wire. For an "ideal" solenoid (infinitely long, tightly packed), the magnetic field is uniform and parallel to the axis inside, and practically zero outside.

Let the solenoid have 'N' total turns over a length 'L'. Let 'n' be the number of turns per unit length (n = N/L). The current is 'I'.

Step 1: Choose Amperian Loop
We choose a rectangular loop (1-2-3-4) of length 'l' and width 'w'. Side 1 is perfectly parallel to the axis inside the solenoid. Side 3 is outside the solenoid. Sides 2 and 4 cut across the coils perpendicular to the field.
Step 2: Evaluate the Line Integral in four parts
∮ B · dl = ∫1 B·dl + ∫2 B·dl + ∫3 B·dl + ∫4 B·dl

- Side 1 (Inside): B and dl are parallel. Integral = B · l
- Sides 2 & 4 (Crossing): B is perpendicular to dl (dot product = 0). Integral = 0
- Side 3 (Outside): B ≈ 0 outside an ideal solenoid. Integral = 0

Summing them up: ∮ B · dl = Bl + 0 + 0 + 0 = Bl
Step 3: Calculate Enclosed Current
The rectangular loop encloses a certain number of wire turns. Since there are 'n' turns per unit length, a length 'l' encloses (n × l) turns. Each turn carries current I.
Iencl = (n × l) × I
Step 4: Apply Ampere's Law
Bl = μ0 (n · l · I)
Final Result:
Cancel the length 'l' on both sides to get:
B = μ0 n I
(Or B = μ0(N/L)I ). Notice the field is independent of the solenoid's radius!
